ARTEFACTS

Security and risk management

Below are a compilation of posts and exercises I have completed during this module.

INITIAL POST

Read Spring et al (2021) and then answer the following questions:

  • What characteristics of CVSS do the authors criticise? Do you agree with the critique? Justify your answer with academic references.
  • The authors also discuss a number of alternatives to CVSS. Select one of these alternatives and post an argument for why it should replace CVSS.

    • Spring et al. (2021) describe the Common Vulnerability Scoring System (CVSS) as “a way to capture the principal characteristics of a vulnerability to help organizations properly assess and prioritize vulnerability management processes” (ibid., p. 74).

    Their paper, “Time to Change the CVSS?” discusses the problem areas surrounding the CVSS scoring system. These include lack of justification, failure to account for consequences and scoring severities, not risks.

    Lack of justification

    Spring et al. state that the CVSS uses an unreliable output, my interpretation is that it is somewhat reductionist; in order to get the base score, eight questions are to be answered where qualitative information is converted to quantitive. Not having exhaustive details can leave a lot of room for speculation which can lead to not having a clear plan of action to respond to the vulnerabilities. In addition to “efforts wasted on remediating vulnerabilities that will never pose a risk to the organization because they will never be exploited” (Capagna, 2020).

    Scoring severity and not risks

    The authors mention that the CVSS design is flawed, as it does not go into sufficient detail about the relationship between risk and vulnerability and how quickly the risk should be responded to. I agree with the statement, as mistaken prioritisation can greatly impact an organisation. Jacobs et al. (2020) express the importance of effectively evaluating risks and vulnerabilities it poses to “a single firm, or to sectors of critical infrastructure” (ibid., p. 11). Furthered by Maury (2022), who suggests that rectifying the security vulnerability is the first step, but a “bug bounty and regular pen tests can increase the effectiveness of vulnerability management”.

    Assessing risks is integral as it involves a more intricate analysis that takes into account a wide range of aspects, including the likelihood of an attack, the potential damage that could be caused, the value of the asset being protected, and the cost of mitigating the vulnerability - all of which organisations and individuals should consider to rectify the vulnerability or attack.

    Alternatives to CVSS

    Despite the flaws addressed in the paper, an alternative to CVSS as a risk assessment model can be DREAD (Damage, Reproducibility, Exploitability, Affected Users and Discoverability). This is used to score the severity of a vulnerability based on five factors: the potential damage it can cause, the likelihood of it being exploited, how easy it is to discover, the number of users affected, and the ability to reproduce the vulnerability.

    Other authors have suggested a hybrid approach that still considers the framework of CVSS and the Vulnerability rating and scoring system (VRSS). Singh & Sharma (2018) suggest this as it provides “both [a] qualitative and quantitative score for prioritization” (ibid., p.37). However, they conclude that both CVSS and VRSS do not account for “time-based metrics” (p.42) which is important so you can see trends and their severity over time and is more practical to real-life events.
