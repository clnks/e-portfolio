    Case Study: Abusive Workplace Behaviour

  • What characteristics of CVSS do the authors criticise? Do you agree with the critique? Justify your answer with academic references.
  • The authors also discuss a number of alternatives to CVSS. Select one of these alternatives and post an argument for why it should replace CVSS.

    • The Association for Computing Machinery's Committee on Professional Ethics (ACM) and the British Computer Society (BCS) serve as key bodies in guiding ethical conduct and professional standards within the computing industry. The scenario involving Max (a technical team lead), Diane (a team member), and Jean (a manager) within an IT team, the principles and guidelines outlined show concerns in social, ethical, legal, and professional aspects.

    Spring et al. state that the CVSS uses an unreliable output, my interpretation is that it is somewhat reductionist; in order to get the base score, eight questions are to be answered where qualitative information is converted to quantitive. Not having exhaustive details can leave a lot of room for speculation which can lead to not having a clear plan of action to respond to the vulnerabilities. In addition to “efforts wasted on remediating vulnerabilities that will never pose a risk to the organization because they will never be exploited” (Capagna, 2020).

    Emotional abuse in the workplace is characterised by repetitive, harmful behaviours that lack recognition of an individual's integrity and violate standards of conduct, often occurring in the presence of a power differential and not necessarily requiring intent by the abuser (Chu, 2014; Godfrey et al., 2020).

    Assessing risks is integral as it involves a more intricate analysis that takes into account a wide range of aspects, including the likelihood of an attack, the potential damage that could be caused, the value of the asset being protected, and the cost of mitigating the vulnerability - all of which organisations and individuals should consider to rectify the vulnerability or attack.

    Max's claiming credit for collaborative work and his hostile treatment of female colleagues raises ethical and legal concerns. In the UK, claiming sole authorship of joint creations might infringe on the copyright owned by the employer, and his behaviour towards female team members could be considered harassment under the Equality Act 2010. This violates professional codes like the ACM's principle of credit acknowledgement and the BCS's emphasis on respect. Beyond Max's actions, his treatment of female colleagues highlights potential issues with gender equality in the workplace (ACM 1.5, 2018., BCS 1.c., n.d.), but also highlights potential gender inequality within the workplace.

    The scenario illustrates the impact of workplace culture on employee well-being and organisational effectiveness. Jean's passive response to Diane's concerns perpetuates a toxic environment by not addressing Max's negative behaviours (Gunderman & Sechris, 2019; Ceacir et al., 2023), contrary to the principles of respect and professionalism highlighted by both ACM (3.3, 2018) and BCS (2.f., n.d). Psychological insights further clarify the damaging effects of these dynamics on individual motivation, team cohesion, and organisational performance, stressing the importance of proactive intervention and cultural change (Rassol et al., 2020; 2021).

    Both the ACM and BCS Codes emphasise the significance of professional competence, integrity, and accountability in computing and psychological practice, respectively. Max's failure to acknowledge the contributions of his team members and Jean's complacency in addressing workplace issues display lapses in professional responsibility.

    By upholding standards of leadership, ethical conduct, and psychological well-being, computing professionals can foster a culture of innovation and inclusivity, in line with the principles articulated in both ethical codes.

    In conclusion, the scenario involving Max, Diane, and Jean stresses the critical importance of following ethical, legal, and professional standards within computing teams. By examining the actions and dynamics within the scenario through the guidelines and code of conduct of the Association for Computing Machinery's Committee on Professional Ethics (ACM) and the British Computer Society (BCS), practical understandings occur regarding the complexities of ethical decision-making and the importance of promoting a culture of integrity and accountability within the computing sector.

    On the other hand, Max's behaviour highlights the ethical challenges inherent in leadership roles, where technical expertise must be offset with principles of fairness, transparency, and respect for others. Diane's experience shows the real-world implications of workplace dynamics managed by ethical principles, highlighting the need for organisations to proactively address issues of discrimination, harassment, and toxic behaviour.

    Jean's response shows the broader challenges faced by managers in upholding ethical standards and fostering a supportive work environment, emphasising the importance of leadership in promoting ethical conduct and organisational culture.
